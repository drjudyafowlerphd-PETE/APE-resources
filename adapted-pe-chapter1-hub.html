Meaning of Adapted Physical Education

Core definitions and terminology

Definition

Adapted Physical Education is an individualized program that includes:

  • Physical and motor fitness
  • Fundamental motor skills and patterns
  • Skills in aquatics and dance
  • Individual and group games and sports

Designed to meet the unique needs of individuals with varying abilities.

Key Terminology

Adapted vs. Adaptive

Adapted: "To adjust" or "to fit" - refers to modifications to meet student needs

Adaptive: Describes a person's behavior - should NOT be used interchangeably

Age Range

Adapted PE focuses on ages 0 to 21, though it may exceed minimum time required by law.

Program Components

  • Educational: Takes place in schools and educational agencies
  • Draws on related services: Medical services, physical therapy, occupational therapy
  • Should NOT be supplanted by: Physical therapy, intramurals, sport days, athletics
  • Program vs. Placement: APE is a program, directly influenced by placement setting

Adapted Physical Activity

Broader term encompassing comprehensive study of physical activity for education, sport, and leisure of individuals with unique needs across the total lifespan.

üèÄ

Adapted Sport

Sport modified to meet unique needs

Definition

Adapted Sport: Sport modified or created to meet the unique needs of individuals.

Examples

  • Basketball (general sport) ‚Üí Wheelchair Basketball (adapted sport)
  • Goalball: A game created for people with visual impairments where players attempt to roll a ball that emits a sound across their opponents' goal

Terminology Preference

"Adapted sport" terminology is preferred for many reasons:

  • Consistent with terms "adapted physical education" and "adapted physical activity"
  • Focuses on modification of sport rather than disability
  • Encourages participation in most integrated environment
  • Consistent with normalization theory
  • Promotes creation of sport opportunities
  • Provides opportunity for pursuit of excellence throughout full spectrum of settings

Settings & Participation

Individuals with disabilities may participate in:

  • General sport
  • Adapted sport in unified, segregated, individualized, and parallel settings
  • Educational settings and regional, national, and international competition

Types of Sport Programs

  • Intramural: Within schools, for all enrolled pupils
  • Extramural: Between two or more schools (play days/sport days)
  • Interscholastic: Competition between representatives from different schools
  • Leisure/Recreation: Formal, open, or unstructured programs for wellness, medical, or therapeutic reasons

Planning: Purposes, Aims, Goals, and Objectives

Framework for program development

Purpose of Adapted PE

Primary Purpose: To promote self-actualization

This promotes optimal personal development and contributes to the whole of society.

Consistent with humanism - a philosophy of helping people become fully human and actualize their potential.

SHAPE America's National Physical Education Standards

The physically educated person:

Standard 1: Develops a variety of motor skills

Through learning experiences in physical education, the student develops motor skills across a variety of environments. Motor skills are a foundational part of child development and support the movements of everyday life. The development of motor skills contributes to an individual's physical literacy journey.

Standard 2: Applies knowledge related to movement and fitness concepts

Through learning experiences in physical education, the student uses their knowledge of movement concepts, tactics, and strategies across a variety of environments. This knowledge helps the student become a more versatile and efficient mover. Additionally, the student applies knowledge of health-related and skill-related fitness to enhance their overall well-being. The application of knowledge related to various forms of movement contributes to an individual's physical literacy journey.

Standard 3: Develops social skills through movement

Through learning experiences in physical education, the student develops the social skills necessary to exhibit empathy and respect for others, and foster and maintain relationships. In addition, the student develops skills for communication, leadership, cultural awareness, and conflict resolution in a variety of physical activity settings.

Standard 4: Develops personal skills, identifies personal benefits of movement, and chooses to engage in physical activity

Through learning experiences in physical education, the student develops an understanding of how movement is personally beneficial and subsequently chooses to participate in physical activities that are personally meaningful (e.g., activities that offer social interaction, cultural connection, exploration, choice, self-expression, appropriate levels of challenge, and added health benefits). The student develops personal skills including goal setting, identifying strengths, and reflection to enhance their physical literacy journey.

Program Framework

Development accomplished through experiences in three domains:

  • Psychomotor Domain: Physical and motor fitness, fundamental motor skills, rhythm/dance, aquatics, games/sports
  • Cognitive Domain: Education OF the psychomotor domain
  • Affective Domain: Development THROUGH the psychomotor domain

Content Goal Areas

Five content areas consistent with IDEA definition:

  1. Physical fitness
  2. Motor development
  3. Rhythm and dance
  4. Aquatics
  5. Games and sports

Service Providers

Key professionals ensuring quality experiences

Key Point

Adapted PE and sport is provided not only by specialists but also by general physical educators.

If only specialists provided services, relatively few students would receive them due to limited number of specialists.

Service Provider Roles

  • Teachers of Physical Education: Must assume responsibility for all children and be willing to contribute to each student's development
  • Coaches: Provide sport instruction and training
  • Therapists: Physical and occupational therapists provide related services
  • Paraeducators: Assist in program delivery
  • Volunteers: Support program implementation

Professional Philosophy

Success for all students requires an instructor with:

  • Appropriate professional knowledge
  • Skills
  • Values
  • Philosophy that looks toward human service beyond win-loss records

Teacher Qualities

Good teachers/coaches display:

  • Caring and helping attitude
  • Recognition of importance of positive self-esteem
  • Acceptance, empathy, friendship, and warmth
  • Creation of secure and controlled learning environment
  • Use of beneficial teaching approaches and styles
  • Individualized and personalized instruction
  • Positive educational environment where all students are accepted and supported

Brief History of Adapted Physical Education

Evolution from medical treatment to comprehensive education

Historical Timeline

1838

Beginning of Adapted Physical Activity

Physical activity began receiving special attention at the Perkins School for students with visual disabilities in Boston. Samuel Gridley Howe advocated for health benefits of physical activity.

Before 1900

Medical Orientation

Physical education was medically oriented and preventive, developmental, or corrective. Influenced by Swedish medical gymnastics system developed by Per Henrik Ling (introduced to US in 1894). Luther Halsey Gulick was a pioneer in promotion of medical gymnastics.

1930s-1950s

Shift to Sport and Whole Person

Programs shifted from medically oriented training to sport-centered PE. Concern for the whole child emerged. Compulsory PE in public schools increased dramatically.

1950s

Emerging Comprehensive Subdiscipline

Greater diversity in students came greater diversity in programs. In 1952, AAHPER formed committee to define subdiscipline and give direction.

1952

AAHPER Committee Definition

Adapted PE defined as "a diversified program of developmental activities, games, sports, and rhythms suited to interests, capacities, and limitations of students with disabilities who may not safely or successfully engage in unrestricted participation in rigorous activities of the regular physical education program."

1954

First Textbook

Arthur Daniels (Ohio State University) authored first textbook entitled Adapted Physical Education. This term still serves as comprehensive term for the subdiscipline.

1960s-Present

Recent and Current Status

Major advances continued with more humanistic, informed, and less discriminatory society. Key developments:

  • Mid-1960s: Concern for people with emotional/learning disabilities impacted adapted PE
  • National Institute of Mental Health funded Buttonwood Farms Project (valuable for recognizing importance of physical activity for those with disabilities)
  • Use of perceptual-motor activities gained attention
  • 1965: Joseph P. Kennedy, Jr. Foundation awarded grant to AAHPERD to launch Project on Recreation and Fitness for the Mentally Retarded
  • 1968: Unit name changed to Unit on Programs for the Handicapped (directed by Dr. Julian U. Stein)
  • Unit dramatically influenced adapted PE at every level throughout the US

Key Historical Figures

  • Samuel Gridley Howe - School director who advocated health benefits of physical activity (1838)
  • Per Henrik Ling - Developed Swedish medical gymnastics system
  • Luther Halsey Gulick - Pioneer in promotion of medical gymnastics
  • Arthur Daniels - Authored first textbook on Adapted PE (1954)
  • Dr. Julian U. Stein - Directed AAHPERD Unit on Programs for the Handicapped

Inclusion Movement

Education in general settings with peers

Definition

Inclusion means educating students with disabilities in a general educational setting.

Movement Origins

The inclusion movement was encouraged by and is compatible with the LRE (Least Restrictive Environment) provisions associated with IDEA.

Education in the LRE requires that children with disabilities be educated alongside children without disabilities to the maximum extent appropriate.

Continuum of Alternative Environments

A continuum of alternative instructional placements may be used for education if those are the most appropriate environments, including:

  • Fully integrated (general education environments)
  • Completely segregated (separate classes/schools)
  • Various options in between

Current Reality

Today, all but a small percentage of students with disabilities attend schools with peers without disabilities.

Over 90% of children with disabilities spend at least part of their school day in general education classrooms.

Philosophy

Many believe that:

  • Separate education is NOT an equal education
  • The setting of a program significantly influences the program
  • Inclusion promotes integration and acceptance

Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

UDL has roots in the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990. It ensures that all students, including those with unique needs, have success in learning, especially in inclusive educational settings.

Litigation

Landmark court cases

Impact of Litigation

Much has been written about the impact of litigation on the guarantee of full educational opportunity in the United States.

Key Cases

Brown v. Board of Education of Topeka (1954)

Significance: Established that the doctrine of "separate but equal" in public education resulted in segregation that violated constitutional rights of Black students.

Impact: Most prominent case serving as important precedent for civil litigation. While not disability-focused, it established the foundation for educational equality.

Pennsylvania Association for Retarded Children v. Commonwealth of Pennsylvania (1972)

Basis: Equal protection and due process clauses of Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments

Key Rulings:

  • Labeling a child as mentally retarded or denying public education/placement in regular setting without due process or hearing violates individual rights
  • All mentally retarded children are capable of benefiting from a program of education and training
  • Mental age may not be used to postpone or deny access to free public program of education and training
  • Having undertaken to provide free, appropriate education, a state may not deny mentally retarded children the same

Mills v. Board of Education of the District of Columbia (1972)

Action: Brought on behalf of seven children to restrain DC from excluding children with disabling conditions from public schools or denying them publicly supported education

Ruling: By failing to provide the children and the class they represented with publicly supported specialized education, the district violated:

  • Controlling statutes
  • Its own regulations
  • Due process

Requirements: DC required to provide publicly supported education, appropriate equitable funding, and procedural due process rights

Litigation Period (1972-1975)

From 1972 to 1975, 46 right-to-education cases related to people with disabilities were tried in 28 states.

These cases provided foundation for much of the legislation to be discussed in the next section.

Laws Important to Adapted Physical Education and Sport

Federal legislation overview

Overview

Four laws or parts of laws and their amendments have had significant impact on adapted PE and adapted sport:

  1. IDEA (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act)
  2. Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973
  3. Olympic and Amateur Sports Act
  4. Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)

Plus: Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) of 2015

Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA)

PL 108-446 (Originally PL 94-142, Education for All Handicapped Children Act of 1975)

Key Highlights of IDEA:

  • Right to free and appropriate education
  • Physical education must be made available to children with disabilities
  • Equal opportunity for nonacademic and extracurricular activities
  • Individualized program designed to meet needs of children with disabilities
  • Programs conducted within the LRE
  • Nondiscriminatory testing and objective criteria for placement
  • Due process
  • Related services to assist in special education

Free, Appropriate Public Education

Special education and related services must be:

  • Provided at public expense, under public supervision and direction, without charge
  • Meet standards of state educational agency
  • Include preschool, elementary, or secondary school education
  • Provided in conformity with an IEP

Definition of Physical Education in IDEA

Physical education = development of:

  • (a) Physical and motor fitness
  • (b) Fundamental motor skills and patterns
  • (c) Skills in aquatics, dance, and individual and group games and sports (including intramural and lifetime sports)

This term includes special PE, adapted PE, movement education, and motor development.

Key Terms Under IDEA

Child with a disability: A child with intellectual disability, hearing impairment including deafness, speech or language impairment, visual impairment including blindness, serious emotional disturbance, orthopedic impairment, autism, traumatic brain injury, learning disability, deafblindness, or multiple disabilities or other health impairments requiring special education and related services.

At-risk infant or toddler: A child under age 3 who would be at risk of experiencing a substantial developmental delay if early intervention services were not provided.

Infants and toddlers: Children under age 3 who need early intervention services because they are experiencing developmental delays or have a diagnosed condition with high probability of resulting in developmental delay.

Program Planning

IEP (Individualized Education Program): For students ages 3-21 with disabilities identified under IDEA. Physical education must be considered and might include specially designed instruction.

IFSP (Individualized Family Service Plan): For infants and toddlers with disabilities. Though PE services not mandated for this age group, they may be offered.

IPEP (Individualized Physical Education Program): Not covered by federal law but recommended for those with unique needs who have not been identified by school as having a disability.

Least Restrictive Environment (LRE)

Students with disabilities are educated alongside students without disabilities to maximum extent appropriate. Special classes, separate schooling, or other removal from general environment occurs only when nature or severity of disability is such that education in general classes with use of supplementary aids and services cannot be achieved satisfactorily.

Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973

PL 93-112

Key Provision: No otherwise qualified person with a disability, solely by reason of that disability, be excluded from participation in, denied benefits of, or subjected to discrimination under any program or activity receiving federal financial assistance.

Focus on Student Needs and Opportunities

IDEA implicitly, if not explicitly, encourages educators to focus on educational needs of student instead of clinical or diagnostic labels.

Equal Opportunity

Section 504 requires equal opportunity to attain same results, gain same benefits, or reach same levels of achievement as peers without disabilities.

Equally Effective vs. Identical

Example: A written test given at end of semester would not provide student who is blind with equal opportunity to demonstrate knowledge of material. An oral or braille exam would provide equivalent (not identical) services.

Most Inclusive Settings Possible

To maximum degree possible, individuals with disabilities should participate in LRE, represented by a continuum of alternative instructional placements.

Section 504 Plan

For students with disabilities not classified under IDEA but who still need accommodations. Example: A 504 plan related to PE might seek specialized instruction or equipment, auxiliary aids or services, or program modifications.

Olympic and Amateur Sports Act

Amateur Sports Act (ASA) of 1978 (PL 95-606)

Amended by Ted Stevens Olympic and Amateur Sports Act of 1998 (PL 105-277)

Has contributed significantly to provision of amateur athletic activity in US, including competition for athletes with disabilities.

Key Provisions

  • Established United States Olympic Committee (USOC) with exclusive jurisdiction over US participation and organization of Olympic Games, Paralympic Games, and Pan American Games
  • USOC encourages and provides assistance to amateur athletic programs and competition for athletes with disabilities
  • Where feasible, expansion of opportunities for meaningful participation in programs of athletic competition for athletes without disabilities

Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA)

PL 101-336 (1990)

Scope: Extended civil rights protection in all areas of life for individuals with disabilities.

Coverage: Employment, public accommodations and services, public transportation, and telecommunications.

Impact on Adapted PE and Sport

In relation to adapted PE and sport, this legislation requires that community recreational facilities (including health and fitness facilities) be accessible and, where appropriate, that reasonable accommodations be made for individuals with disabilities.

Physical educators must develop and offer programs that allow individuals with disabilities to participate in physical activity and sport experiences within the community.

Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA)

PL 114-95 (December 2015)

Replaced Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA) of 1965 and No Child Left Behind (NCLB) Act of 2001 as framework for elementary and secondary education.

Significance for PE

  1. Reaffirms importance of educating children with disabilities consistent with IDEA
  2. Health and PE included as part of "well-rounded education" - definition of "core academic subject" did NOT include health and PE, enabling school districts to access funding through state departments of education

Title IV - Safe and Healthy Students

Funding for Safe and Healthy Students opportunity includes health, PE, and physical activity. After 2016, funding replaced Physical Education Program (PEP) grant funding.

Designed to encourage development of 21st-century community learning centers, school and community partnerships, and funds for after-school programs including nutrition education and physical activity.

Assessment Requirements

States must implement high-quality assessments of student progress toward standards that measure overall performance in each public school and performance of poor, minority, disabled, and English learner subgroups.

Potential benefit to health and PE is immeasurable, particularly for in-school and out-of-school PE and physical activity programs.

History of Adapted Sport

Development of sport opportunities

Early Beginnings

Deaf athletes were among first Americans with disabilities to become involved in organized sport at special schools.

1870s

Ohio School for the Deaf became first school for the Deaf to offer baseball

1885

American football introduced

1906

Basketball introduced at Wisconsin School for the Deaf

1907

First telegraphic track meet between Overbrook and Baltimore schools for the blind. Local results mailed to central committee which made comparisons to determine winners.

1924

First International Silent Games (now Deaflympics) - formal international competition established with nine nations gathering in Paris

1945

American Athletic Association of the Deaf (AAAD) established to provide, sanction, and promote competitive sport opportunities for Americans with hearing impairments

Development of Wheelchair Sports

Sir Ludwig Guttmann of Stoke Mandeville, England, is credited with introducing competitive sport as an integral part of rehabilitation of veterans with disabilities.

Late 1940s

Stoke Mandeville Hospital sponsored first recognized games for wheelchair athletes

1949

University of Illinois organized first national wheelchair basketball tournament, resulting in formation of National Wheelchair Basketball Association (NWBA)

Mid-1950s

Ben Lipton founded National Wheelchair Athletic Association (NWAA). Organization sponsored competitive sport at state, regional, and national levels for participants with spinal cord conditions and other conditions requiring wheelchair use

Late 1960s

National Handicapped Sports and Recreation Association (NHSRA) formed by small group of Vietnam veterans. Dedicated to providing year-round sport and recreational opportunities for individuals with disabilities

Other Sport Organizations

During last quarter of 20th century, other national multisport and unisport programs were formed providing expanded sport offerings to increasing number of individuals with disabilities.

Latest opportunities organized for athletes with:

  • Visual impairments
  • Cerebral palsy
  • Closed head injury
  • Stroke
  • Dwarfism
  • Other conditions

Special Olympics

Created by Joseph P. Kennedy, Jr. Foundation to provide and promote athletic competition for individuals with intellectual disabilities.

1968

Special Olympics held first international games at Soldier Field in Chicago

Impact: Special Olympics has served as model sport organization for individuals with disabilities through leadership in direct service, research, training, advocacy, education, and organizational leadership.

Symbol of Special Olympics (gift from former USSR on occasion of 1979 International Special Olympic Games hosted by State University of New York, College at Brockport)

Contemporary Organizations

Disabled Sports USA (formerly NHSRA)

Renamed in 1994, then in 2020 merged with Adaptive Sports USA to form Move United.

Evolution of Sport Organizations

Evolution within US has led to greater involvement in international competition. Many US sport organizations participate in international games and have international counterparts (notably International Paralympic Committee - IPC).

Multisport vs. Unisport Programs

  • Multisport programs: Several sports included as part of these programs
  • Unisport organizations: Centered on single sports (e.g., NWBA)
  • Trend: Sport organizations traditionally offering programs for athletes without disabilities are beginning to organize more opportunities and competition for athletes with disabilities, reducing need for sport organizations focused primarily on types of disability

School-Based Adapted Sport

1992

Minnesota became first state to welcome athletes with disabilities into its state school association, officially sanctioning interschool sport for junior and senior high school students with disabilities

Recent Development

American Association of Adapted Sports Programs (AAASP) - Georgia-based nonprofit organization developed to build interscholastic sport leagues for students with physical disabilities

Has developed model for other programs throughout country to imitate. AAASP helps states and schools provide equitable school-based sport opportunities and has demonstrated it's possible to add adapted sport programs to existing school district extracurricular offerings without creating undue administrative burden

Legislative Impact

Legislation has resulted in:

  • Funds for professional preparation, research, and other special projects
  • Stimulated activity programs for infants and toddlers
  • Dramatic increases in participation in sport programs for individuals with disabilities

Impact of federal legislation and strong belief in right to and value of education in general educational environment have resulted in significant movement toward inclusion regarding education of children with disabilities in United States.

Society of Health and Physical Educators (SHAPE America)

Formerly: American Alliance for Health, Physical Education, Recreation and Dance (AAHPERD)

Significance: Important national organization making significant contributions to programs for special populations.

Over the years, publications, conferences, and conventions have given much attention to adapted PE and sport‚Äînot only on national level but also within state, district, and local affiliates.

Professional conferences and conventions are among best sources of information on adapted PE.

Key Contributions

  • Many members whose primary professional concern lies in adapted PE and sport
  • Extensive publications and resources
  • National, state, district, and local conferences
  • Professional development opportunities

National Consortium for Physical Education for Individuals with Disabilities (NCPEID)

Also known as: The Consortium

Established: To promote, stimulate, and encourage professional preparation and research

Origins: Started informally in late 1960s by small group of college and university directors of federally funded professional preparation or research projects seeking to share information

Key Activities

  • Extensive backgrounds and interests in adapted PE and sport
  • Provided leadership and input on national issues and concerns
  • Development of IDEA and its rules and regulations
  • Federal funding for professional preparation, research, demonstration projects, and other special projects
  • Monitoring of legislation
  • Annual meetings
  • Active website maintenance

National Standards

The Consortium is recognized for establishing National Standards in Adapted Physical Education as well as a qualifying certification examination.

Those who successfully complete examination are awarded certification in adapted physical education.

International Federation for Adapted Physical Activity (IFAPA)

State Organizations
